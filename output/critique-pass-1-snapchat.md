# Critique — snapchat-advertiser-2015, Pass 1 (sales/adoption)

## Room note
Submitted as an investor deck; it's actually an advertiser sales deck, and it's critiqued as one, per your call. Nothing below counts a missing team slide, market sizing, or an ask against it — a sales deck isn't supposed to carry them.

## Spine check
The deck opens with its conclusion and doesn't earn it until slide 14 — by which point it reads as decoration, not argument. Slide 1 says "Snapchat is the best way to reach 13 to 34 year-olds." Slide 14 is the one that actually proves something: 13-to-34s watch Live Stories up to 8x more than TV for comparable events. That's the fact that makes a buyer's current TV plan feel wrong, and it's the natural opening — instead the deck spends seven slides proving Snapchat has an audience, tours the ad products, and only then gets to the reason any of it matters. The same thing happens with slide 12's vertical-video shift, used to justify a format instead of explaining what stranded the buyer's existing video library. Both halves of the argument already exist in this deck. They're just in the wrong order — this is exactly the pattern DocSend measured: a feature-led deck converted 17.5% of readers to the end; the same content reordered around the buyer converted 65.4%. The fix is sequence, not new material — everything needed is already sitting on slides 12 and 14.

## Slide notes

### Slide 1 — opening
"Snapchat is the best way to reach 13 to 34 year-olds." This is the deck's central claim, and the only slide that could actually prove it — slide 14 — sits at the very end. A media buyer's first questions are "best against what?" and "why is what I'm doing now failing?" This slide answers neither, so it reads as a boast instead of news. What would a buyer need to already believe about their own spend for this line to land as information rather than a claim?

### Slide 5 — "Why Brands Love Snapchat"
Supported by quotes from Daily Mail's CEO, CNN Digital's GM, and an AdAge writer. Not one of them is an advertiser who bought space here and measured a result — two are media partners whose own business benefits from praising the platform, and the third is trade press. This is presented as the deck's social proof, and a buyer evaluating real spend discounts a partner's praise almost entirely. Of the advertisers who've actually run campaigns, which one would go on record — and why isn't that person on this slide instead?

### Slide 8 — "Ads that Work / Furious 7"
"3X likelihood that a Snapchatter who watched the ad saw the movie." The comparison is between people who chose to watch the ad and people who never saw it, matched only on age — so wanting to see the movie could easily explain both the watching and the ticket. This is the strongest number in the deck, and an agency analyst will spot the selection problem in seconds. Was the control matched on intent to see the film, or only on demographics? The same slide puts "93% liked or loved it" right next to it — a soft opinion number crowding a hard outcome number. If you kept only one, which survives a CFO's read of the campaign?

### Slide 11 — "Views"
"100% Viewable, Full-Screen, with Full Attention" — footnoted "simulated screenshot." Viewability and full-screen are things you can measure; "full attention" is a claim about what's in someone's head, and it's the one of the three backed by a mockup instead of a number. Attention is exactly what a buyer thinks they're paying a premium for, so it's the claim they'll press hardest — and bundling an unmeasured one with two real ones risks all three getting discounted together. Which of the three could you put a real number against today?

### Slide 13 — "3V Guarantee"
"Always Plays with Sound" — footnoted "when device audio is turned on." The footnote quietly takes back the word the whole row is built on, and this is the deck's one head-to-head comparison table, where precision matters most. A buyer reading a competitive table is actively hunting for the asterisk. Under what share of real sessions does that condition actually hold — and does the honest version of that row still beat the columns next to it? The same table leaves Facebook and YouTube blank against three "Always" claims, and a buyer who runs campaigns on both platforms knows YouTube does offer full-screen mobile video — an overstated blank next to something they can check personally costs credibility on the claims they can't check.

### Slide 16 — "FAQ"
The deck closes on five answered objections and nothing asked for — no pilot, no contact, no first campaign. This is the slide people sit on and forward, and ending on defense means the buyer has to invent their own next step, which they won't. What's the smallest, lowest-risk first campaign a brand could commit to right now? Buried in this same FAQ is the deck's biggest real objection — recutting ads costs actual money — answered with "it's worth it" and an "up to 9x" figure from Snapchat's own data, on a slide that's turned sideways. The cost side never gets a number while the benefit side does. What did recutting actually cost on the Furious 7 campaign, and does "up to nine times" still sound persuasive next to that real figure?

## What's working
Slide 7's age-band chart lands the "young audience" point in about two seconds — greying out the older brackets makes the case before you read a single number. This is what the whole deck should be doing. Slide 8's Millward Brown data, selection issue aside, is the only outcome in the deck measured by a third party against a named campaign — it's the seed of a real proof section, just alone right now. Slide 12 is cleanly sourced (eMarketer) and describes a genuine shift in the world, independent of Snapchat — that's the hardest test to pass and it passes it; its only fault is where it sits. And the deck is honest about its own numbers throughout — even the unflattering ones are labeled "Snapchat internal data" rather than dressed up as independent, which is rarer than it should be and earns trust for the numbers that are third-party.

## Score
As designed: a 5.7 out of 10 — real pieces, but two gaps (no cost of inaction, no call to action) trip the sales rubric's automatic ceiling, so it can't read as complete yet. As the file stands: 4.7 — the FAQ slide is rotated sideways and a couple of images are cropped at the edges, most likely from how this copy was re-exported rather than Snapchat's own file.

**Worth fixing first:**
1. Open with the buyer's problem, not Snapchat's scale. Slides 12 and 14 already have what's needed — this is pure reordering.
2. Put a number on what staying with TV costs a brand. Slide 14 shows the shift; nothing turns it into a cost.
3. End on a next step. The FAQ shouldn't be the last thing a buyer reads.

## Appendix — presentation prep
No items this pass. Every load-bearing number here is already addressed as a slide finding above (the selection problem, the unmeasured "attention" claim, the footnote reversal) — nothing is being quietly under-supported that isn't already named.

## About the A1 score (why 1 out of 3, not 0)
It's a 1, not a 0, because the deck does hold the product back — ad formats don't appear until slide 9, with eight slides of audience and proof first. That restraint is real and it's why the score isn't the floor.

The actual gap: no slide ever establishes that the buyer has a problem. Slide 1 has to open on a conclusion because there's no problem yet to draw it from, and the six slides after it are all about Snapchat's own scale — the advertiser only shows up as a spectator.

That single gap drags down a lot else. Something like 40% of the points lost on this pass trace back to it: you can't cost a problem you never named, the "losers" in this shift are brands still buying only TV but that fact is buried at slide 14 where it can't do its job, and the product slides read as a catalogue because no obstacle was ever set up for them to remove.

The fix has a cheap half and an expensive half. The cheap half is pure reordering — no new material. Slides 12 and 14 already exist and are already sourced; move them to the front, and let the current opening scale numbers become the answer to "why does this matter" instead of the opening claim. That alone moves this to a 2 out of 3.

The expensive half is adding the step that turns the shift into a real cost for this specific buyer — what a brand still buying TV against 13-to-34s is actually losing. Slide 14 shows the audience has moved; nothing shows what staying put costs. That needs a number the deck doesn't have yet — delivery-per-dollar on a TV buy against this demo, and how far it's fallen. Both fixes together are worth more than anything else on the sheet.

One fairness note: in 2015, vertical mobile video wasn't a real budget line yet, and Snapchat still had a credibility problem with these buyers. Opening with "your TV spend is failing," from a company media planners didn't yet take seriously, might reasonably have gotten dismissed by slide 3 — so leading with scale may have been the right read of that specific moment. Take the 2-out-of-3 read as solid; don't hold the 1 against them too hard.

One more thing: the sales rubric doesn't have any benchmarked decks yet, so treat the 5.7 as provisional — the list of what to fix is trustworthy already, the exact number needs a couple more decks before it's fully calibrated.
