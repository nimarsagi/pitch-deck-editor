# Critique — coinbase-zlides-reconstruction, Pass 1 (investor)

## Provenance — read before the critique
This isn't the deck the rubric benchmarks, and the difference isn't cosmetic. Slide 7 — "the secure app to store crypto yourself," "use decentralized apps" — is Coinbase Wallet, roughly 2018; in 2012 Coinbase was a bitcoin-only buy/sell service. Slides 3 and 6 show logos including Ethereum, CryptoKitties, and Tether — none of which existed in 2012. Slide 8's screenshot shows bitcoin around $9,512; it traded around $10 in 2012. Slide 11 credits Brian Armstrong with GiveCrypto.org, founded 2018. Against that, slides 4, 9, and 10 carry figures that do look like real 2012-era Coinbase numbers. So this file is a hybrid — original traction wrapped in a modern redesign. That means two things: this score is a score of the reconstruction, not of Coinbase's real 2012 raise, because it isn't the same document; and the visual polish this file scores well on (C1) is Zlides' work, not the founders'. Everything below critiques the file as given — a fair critique of this artifact, and a poor stand-in for the original.

## Spine check
The deck sells bitcoin for six slides and never turns to sell Coinbase. Slide 2 opens on the category — "no fees, international, instant" — and slides 3 through 6 stay there: a logo grid, a volume figure, a list of use cases, another logo grid. The company's own product doesn't show up until slide 7, past the halfway point of twelve slides. A cold reader can't say what the company does by slide 3 here — they can't say it by slide 6. Everything before slide 7 argues that bitcoin is interesting, which an investor either already believes or doesn't, and if they do, half the deck has just been spent agreeing with them. The traction (slides 9 and 10) is genuinely strong, but it arrives only after the reader has sat through a category explainer first — this is an ordering problem, not a content one.

## Slide notes

### Slide 4 — transaction volume
"$2 million USD per day in transaction volume." Read against slide 10, this doesn't add up — if this is Coinbase's own volume, it implies far more than the $65k the deck states for the first five weeks; if it's bitcoin's network-wide volume instead, nothing marks it as context rather than a company metric. Which is it — and if it's the network's, what on the slide should show that?

### Slide 5 — Early Adopters
Eight use cases — international payments, virtual goods, developing-world payments, games, peer-to-peer, remittance, micro-transactions, e-commerce — listed with equal weight and no market sized behind any of them. This is the slide where a beachhead belongs, and it offers breadth instead of a bet. Which one are your current users actually in, and what would this slide look like if it named only that one?

### Slide 9 — Coinbase Signups
"20% Daily Growth," charted over two weeks starting right where the curve leaves zero — so the rate is measured across the launch spike itself. Run 20% a day for real and you'd multiply roughly 1,000x in six weeks, which can't be right, and the axis label is the only place that limitation shows. Anyone who does that math will conclude you didn't check it — an expensive impression to leave with your best slide. What does the curve look like at week six or eight, and does the honest headline become the shape of the curve rather than the daily rate?

### Slide 11 — Founding Team
Brian Armstrong and Fred Ehrsam, with company logos underneath — Goldman Sachs, Airbnb, and others. The logos gesture at relevance but never say it outright: does Goldman mean you understand moving money, does Airbnb mean you've scaled a two-sided marketplace? Team is one of the two slides investors spend the longest on, and right now it reads like a résumé rather than a case for why the two of you win this specific market. What do you each know about moving money that someone without your background would get wrong — and where would a reader see that here?

### Slide 12 — the close
"coinbase.com — buy and sell cryptocurrency." The last slide is a URL and a one-line product description — no amount, no milestone, no ask of any kind. This is the slide that's supposed to turn interest into a next meeting, and right now it sends a persuaded investor to go sign up as a customer instead. What are you raising, and what milestone does it buy before the next round?

## What's working
Slide 10 is the best-built claim in the deck — "$65k USD transaction in the first 5 weeks" — specific figure, named unit, bounded timeframe, and falsifiable, which is what makes it land. The traction pair (slides 9 and 10) does real work: a growth chart with visible dates next to a concrete transaction number, which is the section most seed investors are hoping to find and most decks can't supply. Slide 2 also does the why-now job efficiently in three words each — no fees, international, instant — a genuine shift, just in the wrong position. And the number slides (2, 9, 10) are all built the right way: the headline makes the claim, the slide then proves it.

## Score
As designed: a 4.8 out of 10 — five sections (market, competition, business model, the ask, and business-clear-by-slide-3) are missing outright. As the file stands: 3.8 — a third-party ad slide, plus the slide 4 / slide 10 numbers that can't both be true.

**Worth fixing first:**
1. Get to what Coinbase actually does by slide 3, not slide 7. Six slides sell bitcoin before the company shows up — this alone is worth more than any other single fix.
2. Add market sizing, competition, and a business model. Right now none of the three appear anywhere in the deck.
3. Add an ask. The deck currently ends on a website address.

## Appendix — presentation prep
No items this pass. The weak spots here are sections missing outright (market, model, competition, the ask) rather than numbers quietly under-supported — there's nothing further to flag beyond what's already named above.

Treat 4.8 as this artifact's score, not as evidence about the deck Coinbase actually raised on.
