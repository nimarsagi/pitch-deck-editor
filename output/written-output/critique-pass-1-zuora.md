# Critique — zuora-subscription-economy, Pass 1 (sales/adoption)

## Spine check
The chain holds, and it holds unusually well. The deck runs a clean category-creation arc: name a shift bigger than the vendor ("WE NOW LIVE IN A SUBSCRIPTION ECONOMY") → make staying put fatal ("IN THE LAST 15 YEARS, 52% OF THE [Fortune 500 have disappeared]" → "WHO SURVIVED THE MASS EXTINCTION?") → show who's winning (GE, Unilever, IBM transformed; Amazon/Google/Uber/Spotify as "the relationship-makers") → hand over the new way of operating (subscriber identity, "#beyondCRM") → and only then, on slide 20, reveal the product as the obvious bridge ("THAT'S WHY WE BUILT ZUORA"). Each slide earns the next; the product arrives as a resolution, not an interruption. The one seam where the chain strains is that same slide 20 — see the first note. The spine itself is intact.

## Slide notes

### Slide 20 — "A unified solution for the Subscription Economy"
Nineteen slides establish one idea at a time, in huge type, on full-bleed imagery — and then the product reveal drops a seven-column grid (Subscriber Management, Pricing & Packaging, Billing, Payments, Quoting, Revenue, Analytics) with three-to-five sub-items each, plus a Platform bar of six more. It's ~40 feature words at the exact moment the room has finally been made to want the product. The deck spent the whole runway teaching the audience to feel one thing per slide, then breaks its own visual language on the slide that matters most, and the buyer's eye has nowhere to land. Live, you can talk the audience through it — but if the goal of this slide is "here is the one thing Zuora does for the world you just described," what is that one thing, and does the grid say it or bury it?

### Slides 10–15 — the promised-land run
Six straight slides make a single point — incumbents transformed and disruptors won by going relationship-first: GE, Unilever/Dollar Shave Club, IBM, then Amazon/Google/Apple/Facebook/Salesforce, then Uber/Spotify/Box/Airbnb. Live, the accumulation is deliberate and it builds pressure. But it's also the one stretch that repeats rather than advances, so it's your timing shock-absorber: if a keynote slot runs short, this is the section that can lose two slides without the argument losing a link. Which two of these six are carrying the point, and which are there for rhythm?

### The altitude of the pain (slides 2–9)
The stakes are drawn at 30,000 feet — "the way people buy has changed," "52% of the Fortune 500 gone." That's exactly right for a category keynote, and it's what makes the room lean back and think big. What it never does is drop to one operator's actual Tuesday — the billing team that can't launch a new pricing plan without engineering, the finance lead who can't close the books because usage revenue won't reconcile. A skeptical buyer feels a market shift intellectually but feels their own pain viscerally, and this deck only gives them the first. Is there one concrete, named operator problem you could land somewhere before slide 20, so the shift stops being about "every industry" and starts being about them?

## What's working
- **The title chain carries the whole argument on its own.** Read only the headlines in order and you get the complete case — "TURNING CUSTOMERS INTO SUBSCRIBERS," "THE WAY PEOPLE BUY HAS CHANGED FOR GOOD," "WHO SURVIVED THE MASS EXTINCTION?", "THAT'S WHY WE BUILT ZUORA." Most decks lean on their bodies where their headlines should carry; this one doesn't. Don't touch the headlines.
- **The "X not Y" triad on slide 4** — "OUTCOMES not Ownership / CUSTOMIZATION not Generalization / CONSTANT IMPROVEMENT not Planned Obsolescence." Three crisp contrasts that do real argumentative work and are impossible to misread. This is the deck's rhetorical engine.
- **The mass-extinction device (slides 8–9).** Costing the shift in dead Fortune 500 companies, then cutting to a bare "WHO SURVIVED THE MASS EXTINCTION?" on empty gray, is the moment the room stops nodding and starts worrying. That's the cost-of-inaction step most decks skip.
- **The proof is named and senior.** Aaron Levie (CEO, Box) — "There was a pre-Zuora Box and a post-Zuora Box" — plus a CTO (FT) and an EVP (Schneider Electric). Real people, real titles, quotable lines. Keep them.

## Score
As a live keynote: **8.5 out of 10** — a near-model category-creation deck; the argument is exemplary and the one real soft spot is the product reveal that breaks its own visual discipline. One caveat, not a deduction: this is built to be narrated. If it ever circulates as a leave-behind with no one presenting, the bare stakes slides go quiet and the soft close ("#startyourstory") stops asking for anything — it would lose roughly a point and a half on the same rubric. As a live deck, that's not its job, so it's not scored against it.

**Worth fixing first:**
1. Give slide 20 a one-line spine before the grid — the single thing Zuora does for the world slides 2–19 just built. The feature grid can stay for reference; it just can't be the first thing the eye hits at peak attention.
2. Land one concrete operator pain before the product. The deck earns the market shift but never makes an individual buyer say "that's my Tuesday."
3. Know which two of slides 10–15 are load-bearing, so you can cut to time without cutting the argument.

## Appendix — presentation prep
- **Item:** "IN THE LAST 15 YEARS, 52% OF THE [Fortune 500 have disappeared]" (slide 8).
  **Likely to come up because:** it's the emotional hinge of the whole stakes section, and a skeptical exec may know the real churn figure and the "disappeared" definition (acquired vs bankrupt vs dropped off the list are very different stories).
  **Be ready to:** say where the figure comes from and what "disappeared" counts as, without putting any of that on the slide.
- **Item:** "$420B ... up from $215B" US subscription/consumption figure (slide 5).
  **Likely to come up because:** it's the number that sizes the "phenomenon," and the source and scope (which spend counts as "subscription") are the first thing an analyst-minded buyer probes.
  **Be ready to:** name the source and what's in and out of the number if asked. It does not belong on the slide.
