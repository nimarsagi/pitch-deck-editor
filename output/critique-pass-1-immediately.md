# Critique — immediately-sales-2015, Pass 1 (sales/adoption)

## Spine check
The deck earns the pain and then never proves it can fix it. Slide 4 sets up a real unit of value — an hour of a rep's time is worth $50 — which is the right move, because it tells the buyer that time recovered turns into money. Slide 9 then claims the payoff: "more time actually selling." Between those two, the deck never supplies the number that connects them — how many hours this actually gives back, to whom, measured where. The same gap shows up as a total absence of proof: there's no customer, no logo, no testimonial, no measured outcome anywhere in ten slides. A deck that's done this good a job making the reader feel the pain then asks them to take the cure entirely on faith. This is one fix, not two — a single real customer with a measured before-and-after would close both holes at once, and it's the highest-leverage thing this deck could add.

## Slide notes

### Slide 2 — response latency
"Companies which contacted people within an hour were sixty times more likely to connect." This is the best insight in the whole deck — a genuine, non-obvious reason speed matters — and it's set in the smallest type on the slide, in a footer, under a photo. The made-up thought bubble above it gets the visual weight instead. A buyer skimming without a rep in the room reads the big text and the image and may never reach the footer. Between the imagined thought and the 60x finding, which do you want them to walk away remembering — and which one currently gets the space?

### Slide 4 — the value of an hour
"An hour of a salesperson's time is worth $50 an hour (assuming a $100K salary)." You state a rate and never turn it into a number — how many hours this actually gives back, measured somewhere. Fifty dollars an hour times nothing isn't a business case, and it leaves the buyer to guess your hours-saved claim on your behalf, usually low. How many hours a week does this actually return, and where did you measure it?

### Slide 5 — positioning
"Our mission is to elevate sales back to its core foundation: a relationship-driven craft." This is the deck's only positioning slide, and it reads like an internal mission statement rather than a plain description — it's also the one slide with no headline at all. It sits right at the hinge between the pain half and the product half, exactly where a reader decides whether to keep going, and where a champion forwarding this deck needs the sentence they'll use to describe you to their VP. If someone saw only this slide, could they tell a colleague what the product does in one sentence — and is that the sentence you want them using?

### Slide 9 — "Immediately Enables…"
Five claims — more selling time, speedier cleanup, automated logging, happier reps, faster response — and not one carries a number. "Speedier" and "faster" are comparisons with no baseline, and this is the last content slide, so it's where the buyer's takeaway sets. For which one of these five do you actually have a measured result from a real user? Worth noting too: "happier account executives" is the only named outcome anywhere in the deck, and it belongs to the end user, not to whoever signs the contract — a VP Sales is judged on forecast accuracy and quota, not rep happiness. Who actually signs off here, and which of these five is the thing they're held accountable for?

### Slide 10 — the close
"Get Started Today," with a name, email, and phone number. This is an invitation, not a step — it could mean downloading an app, booking a call, or starting a rollout, and nothing says what week one looks like. The closing slide is the one people sit on and forward; leaving the next move to invent means most won't bother, especially with no proof yet to justify the effort. What's the smallest commitment a sales team could make on a Friday afternoon, and how soon would they see a first result?

## What's working
The pain is described in a real rep's voice — "all this time updating Salesforce is killing me… I never make it out of the office at a decent hour anymore" — specific system, specific consequence, sounds like a person instead of a marketer. That's the single best thing in this deck; don't touch it. The pain slides and product slides are deliberately paired in order too — slide 6 answers slide 2, slide 7 answers slide 3, slide 8 answers slide 4 — three problems, three answers, matched sequence, which most decks at this stage don't have. Product placement is disciplined as well: nothing about the product until slide 5, no screenshots until slide 6 — four slides of the buyer's world come first. And slides 2 and 3 both cite real sources, which is exactly why slide 4's missing math stands out.

## Score
As designed: a 5.8 out of 10 — the pain is the best part of the deck, but there's no proof anywhere that the fix actually works, which is the sales rubric's automatic ceiling. As the file stands: 5.3 — one citation on slide 3 is truncated and can't be followed; everything else is clean.

**Worth fixing first:**
1. Add one real customer with a measured result. There's no proof anywhere in ten slides — the single biggest gap, and it also finishes the incomplete math from slide 4.
2. Turn the $50/hour rate into an actual cost. State how many hours this gives back and multiply it out.
3. Speak to whoever signs, not just the rep. A per-seat, Salesforce-integrated tool gets approved by a VP Sales or ops, and nothing here is addressed to them.

## Appendix — presentation prep
The $50/hour rate on slide 4 (assuming a $100K salary). It doesn't need to be shown on the slide — but if a buyer asks where that number came from, or whether it holds for their team's pay scale, have that ready.
